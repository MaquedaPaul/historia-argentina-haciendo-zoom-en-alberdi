# Feature Research — M023: Alto Peru, Guemes, and the Republiquetas

**Domain:** Historical content cards — Argentine wars of independence (military front, 1810-1821)
**Researched:** 2026-03-26
**Confidence:** HIGH (battle dates and outcomes from Wikipedia/ES verified against multiple academic sources; interpretive framing MEDIUM)

## What SP1-3 Already Covers (Do Not Duplicate)

Card SP1-3 "Belgrano en el Norte, San Martín en Cuyo" (1812–1816) is a **panoramic summary card** with no expand/collapse section. It covers:

- Belgrano created the flag (27 Feb 1812)
- Tucumán victory (24 Sept 1812) — mentioned but not explained
- Salta victory (20 Feb 1813) — mentioned but not explained
- San Martín requested Cuyo governorship (1814)
- San Martín prepared the Army of the Andes over three years

**What SP1-3 does NOT cover** (safe to build):
- The Éxodo Jujeño (Aug 1812) — the human/strategic story behind the Tucumán victory
- What happened AFTER Salta: the disasters at Vilcapugio and Ayohuma
- The First Expedition (1810-1811): Castelli, Suipacha, Huaqui
- The Third Expedition (1815): Sipe-Sipe
- Why San Martín's choice of Chile was a direct consequence of northern failures
- Güemes and the guerra gaucha
- The republiquetas and their leaders
- The connective narrative arc: north failed → San Martín went via Chile

## The Four Expeditions to Alto Peru — Event Map

### First Expedition (1810-1811), commander: Juan José Castelli / Balcarce

| Event | Date | Outcome |
|-------|------|---------|
| Battle of Cotagaita | 27 Oct 1810 | Royalist victory (minor) |
| Battle of Suipacha | 7 Nov 1810 | **Patriot victory — first victory of the revolution** |
| Battle of Huaqui | 20 Jun 1811 | Royalist decisive victory — "the disaster" |

### Second Expedition (1812-1813), commander: Manuel Belgrano

| Event | Date | Outcome |
|-------|------|---------|
| Éxodo Jujeño | 23 Aug 1812 | Tactical earth-scorched retreat enabling Tucumán |
| Battle of Tucumán | 24 Sept 1812 | Patriot victory — saved the revolution |
| Battle of Salta | 20 Feb 1813 | Patriot victory — Belgrano's peak |
| Battle of Vilcapugio | 1 Nov 1813 | Royalist victory — collapse begins |
| Battle of Ayohuma | 14 Nov 1813 | Royalist decisive victory — Belgrano relieved of command |

### Third Expedition (1815), commander: José Rondeau

| Event | Date | Outcome |
|-------|------|---------|
| Battle of Sipe-Sipe | 29 Nov 1815 | Royalist decisive victory — end of northern strategy |

### Fourth Expedition / Guerra Gaucha phase (1816-1821), Güemes dominant

| Event | Date | Outcome |
|-------|------|---------|
| Battle of Tarija | 15 Apr 1817 | Patriot guerrilla victory |
| De la Serna's main invasion (7,000 troops) | 1816-1817 | Repelled by gaucho tactics |
| Royalist raid on Salta, Güemes wounded | 6 Jun 1821 | Güemes mortally wounded |
| Death of Güemes | 17 Jun 1821 | End of northern defense era |

### Anti-Features (Do Not Build)

| Feature | Why Requested | Why Problematic | Alternative |
|---------|---------------|-----------------|-------------|
| **Full biography card for Belgrano** | Belgrano is central; tempting to do a full profile | SP1-3 and INV-11 already profile Belgrano extensively; duplication without added value; would derail from the Alto Peru focus | Use Belgrano as a throughline character (he appears in Suipacha, Éxodo, Tucumán, Salta, Vilcapugio, Ayohuma, and granting rank to Azurduy) without re-profiling him |
| **Full biography card for San Martín** | SP1-5 already covers the Andes/Chile/Peru campaign | The northern failure is the CAUSE, not the story; the story of the crossing is already told | One connective card "Por qué Chile" is enough; don't re-tell Chacabuco or Maipú |
| **Card for every individual battle in the guerra gaucha** | Huacalera, Las Cañas, Yala, Cuesta de la Pedrera are all real battles | Individual combat cards without narrative thread become a list, not a story | Use one card for the guerra gaucha's tactics and one for major invasions (De la Serna); specific combats become detail in the expand section |
| **Card for Tarija republiqueta (Méndez, Pérez de Uriondo)** | Completeness — the Tarija republiqueta is legitimate history | Its leaders (Eustaquio Méndez, Francisco Pérez de Uriondo) are not in Argentine civic memory; would require heavy contextualization | Mention Tarija in the republiquetas overview card as a sub-entry in the expanded detail section |
| **Card for the Fourth Expedition as a conventional military campaign** | Belgrano commanded a fourth expedition (1816-1822) | Its military story (Tarija, Sopachuy) is subordinate to the Güemes-led guerrilla phase in historical importance; the conventional army is repeatedly outperformed | Frame the fourth expedition as "the army that needed Güemes to survive," not as a separate campaign |

### Dependency Notes

- **Éxodo Jujeño requires Huaqui context:** Without knowing the first expedition failed, the strategic urgency of the Éxodo is unclear.
- **"Por qué Chile" requires Sipe-Sipe:** This card should come after all three northern defeats are established, not before.
- **Juana Azurduy requires Padilla context:** His Sept 1816 death is part of her story; they can share a card or Padilla can be sub-content.
- **Güemes profile requires guerra gaucha tactics card OR the tactics should be in the profile:** They are the same story; one card or a split into profile + tactics is a content design decision, not a dependency issue.

## Narrative Arc — How the Cards Connect

The eight P1 cards form a single narrative arc in two movements:

**Movement 1: The Northern Failure (1810-1815)**
ALTO-01 Suipacha → ALTO-02 Huaqui → ALTO-03 Éxodo Jujeño → [SP1-3 Tucumán/Salta — already exists] → ALTO-04 Vilcapugio+Ayohuma → ALTO-05 Sipe-Sipe → ALTO-06 "Por qué Chile" (bridge to existing SP1-5)

**Movement 2: The Defensive North (1815-1821)**
GUEM-01 Güemes profile → GUEM-02 Azurduy+republiquetas → [GUEM-01 death section closes the arc]

**Alberdi thread:** Alberdi was born in Tucumán in 1810; his father Salvador Alberdi was a patriot and friend of Belgrano; young Alberdi grew up in the shadow of these campaigns. The Éxodo Jujeño and Belgrano's defeats are part of Alberdi's lived historical context as a Tucumán native. This thread is already established in the existing site and should be reinforced in the new cards — particularly in ALTO-03 and the connective arc card.
